> *not* the one on forking a repo. The forking article is when you want to make a copy of someone *else's* repository and work on your copy of it. In your case, it is your own repo that you control. So you need to:
> A) create the repo on GitHub (you did this already)
> B) clone it to your local workstation (that's what "git clone" does; you can also do it via GitHub for Mac or other UI)
> C) hack on the local copy on your workstation
> D) commit your changes, which is like taking a snapshot so you don't lose work
> E) push your commits back to the remote server, so others can see what you did
> 
> Pushing your changes back to GitHub is also nice in case your computer explodes, so you don't lose any work. You won't even lose the *history* of your work since the entire thing is there on the server. (And the entire thing is there on you local workstation -- that's why it's called a "clone").
